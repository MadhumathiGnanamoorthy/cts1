Pituitary Tumor

What is a Pituitary Tumor?

A pituitary tumor is an abnormal growth in the pituitary gland, a small organ located at the base of the brain. The pituitary gland plays a crucial role in regulating various hormones in the body. Although most pituitary tumors are benign (noncancerous), they can affect hormone production and cause various health issues.

Pituitary tumors account for about 15% of all primary brain tumors. They can occur at any age but are most commonly diagnosed in older adults.

Types of Pituitary Tumor

  • Functioning Tumors: These tumors produce excess hormones, leading to a variety of symptoms depending on the hormone involved. Common types include prolactinomas, growth hormone-secreting tumors, and ACTH-secreting tumors.
  • Nonfunctioning Tumors: These tumors do not produce hormones and may not cause symptoms until they grow large enough to press on surrounding tissues, including the optic nerves, leading to vision problems.
  • Craniopharyngiomas: Though not technically pituitary tumors, these benign tumors occur near the pituitary gland and can cause similar symptoms due to their location.

Symptoms

The symptoms of a pituitary tumor depend on the type of tumor and the hormones it affects. Common symptoms include:

  • Headaches, particularly those that are persistent or severe.
  • Vision problems, such as double vision, blurred vision, or loss of peripheral vision.
  • Unexplained weight gain or loss.
  • Changes in menstrual cycles or erectile dysfunction.
  • Fatigue, weakness, or dizziness.
  • Increased sensitivity to cold or heat.
  • Mood changes, such as depression or anxiety.
  • Excessive thirst and frequent urination.
